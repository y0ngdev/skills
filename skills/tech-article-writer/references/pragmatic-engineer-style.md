## Voice and Tone

- **First-person perspective**: Write as a senior engineer sharing hard-won insights. Use "I" and "we" naturally.
- **Opinionated but evidence-based**: Take clear positions backed by data, experience, or sourced claims. Avoid wishy-washy "it depends" without follow-through.
- **Insider knowledge**: Reference real industry dynamics — how big tech actually works, how hiring really happens, how teams ship. Convey authority through specificity.
- **Conversational but substantive**: Reads like a smart colleague explaining something over coffee, not a textbook. Casual phrasing is fine; shallow analysis is not.
- **Balanced skepticism**: Question hype and conventional wisdom. Present counterarguments fairly before drawing conclusions.
- **Direct address**: Speak to the reader as a peer. "If you've worked at a large company, you've probably seen this."

## Article Structure

### Typical layout

1. **Hook / context setter** (1-3 paragraphs): Start with a timely observation, surprising data point, or industry event. Establish why this matters *now*.
2. **Thesis / framing**: State the core argument or question clearly within the first few paragraphs.
3. **Deep-dive sections** (3-7 sections): Each section covers a distinct facet. Use H2 headers. Sections often follow a pattern of *observation → analysis → implication*.
4. **Industry context**: Weave in comparisons across companies, regions, or time periods. Reference specific companies by name.
5. **Practical takeaways**: End sections or the article with actionable advice for the reader's career or team.
6. **Closing reflection**: A brief, forward-looking conclusion. Often poses an open question or prediction.

### Section length

- Sections run 300-800 words each.
- Total article length: 2,000-5,000 words (long-form is the norm).
- Depth over brevity — don't rush through complex topics.

## Content Patterns

- **Named sources and specifics**: "At Uber, the team had 12 engineers and shipped in 6 weeks" beats "at a large company, a team shipped quickly."
- **Numbered lists for comparisons**: Use numbered lists when comparing approaches, listing reasons, or ranking options.
- **Embedded data**: Include salary figures, survey results, adoption percentages. Cite sources when available.
- **Cross-company analysis**: Compare how 3-5 companies handle the same problem. Name them explicitly.
- **Historical context**: Show how something evolved. "In 2019, most companies did X. By 2024, the shift to Y was clear."
- **Callout boxes / asides**: Use blockquotes or italics for editorial asides, reader tips, or caveats.
- **Reader engagement hooks**: Pose questions mid-article. "Have you seen this at your company?"

## Formatting Conventions

- **Headers**: H1 for title, H2 for major sections, H3 for subsections. Keep headers descriptive and specific ("How Stripe handles on-call" not "On-call").
- **Bold** for key terms on first use and for emphasis in lists.
- **Italics** for publication names, asides, and light emphasis.
- **Blockquotes** for sourced quotes or editorial callouts.
- **Images/diagrams**: Use when they clarify architecture, workflows, or data. Always add captions.
- **Links**: Link to primary sources, company blogs, and prior articles. Inline links preferred.
- **Paragraphs**: Keep to 2-4 sentences. Break for readability.

## Example Openings

**Pattern 1 — News hook:**
> Last week, Google announced layoffs affecting 12,000 employees. This is notable not just for its scale, but for what it signals about how Big Tech is restructuring engineering organizations...

**Pattern 2 — Contrarian observation:**
> Most engineering managers assume that adding more process improves reliability. In my experience across three companies, the opposite was true — and here's why.

**Pattern 3 — Data-driven:**
> A recent survey of 2,500 engineers found that 68% have never negotiated their salary. Having coached dozens of engineers through negotiations, I find this unsurprising — but fixable.

**Pattern 4 — Personal experience:**
> When I joined Uber as an engineering manager in 2016, the on-call system was, to put it mildly, a mess. What we built to fix it taught me more about engineering culture than any management book.
